\section*{Executive Summary}

This white paper documents the current Aeolus engineering analysis of an aircraft-seeded hurricane intervention concept using NH$_4$Cl aerosol generation. The central question is not whether aerosol intervention is atmospherically effective in the abstract; it is whether a physically specified intervention can be supplied by a plausible aircraft system once particle diameter, target number concentration, campaign duration, onboard power, onboard fuel, tanker support, and direct airborne mission cost are all treated together. This paper answers that systems question with a diameter-based payload model, a top-down molten-salt process-power model, a stock-C-130 fuel-budget model, an aerial-refueling support model, and a flight-hour cost model tied to public aircraft references and explicitly labeled engineering assumptions \citep{AeolusPayloadMethods,AeolusPowerNote,AeolusFuelNote,AeolusCostNote,AeolusSourceLog}.

\vspace{0.25em}
With spherical NH$_4$Cl particles, required mass scales as $d^3$, so particle diameter is a first-order driver of every downstream payload, power, and mission-support estimate. The treated storm region is modeled as an annulus rather than a surface-area surrogate, and the campaign requirement is treated as a \emph{continuous saturation problem} over \SI{48}{h}: an initial fill plus replenishment of advected air. Under the baseline geometry, replenishment dominates the one-shot fill term, which means the problem is fundamentally a sustained throughput problem, not an inventory-only problem \citep{AeolusPayloadMethods}.

\vspace{0.25em}
Three top-level results emerge. First, the aerosol burden moves extremely quickly with particle diameter: the full-ring, \SI{2000}{particles/cm^3}, \SI{20}{nm} case requires about \SI{85.7}{t} of NH$_4$Cl over \SI{48}{h}, while the \SI{50}{nm} case rises to about \SI{1339.8}{t}; by contrast, the \SIrange{5}{10}{nm} laboratory band is only about \SIrange{1.34}{10.72}{t} for the same concentration target. Second, once NH$_4$Cl throughput is translated into process power using the molten-salt basis of \SI{1.08}{kWh/kg}, the aircraft problem becomes an electrical-architecture problem as much as a chemistry problem: a \SI{100}{kg/h} aircraft module already implies roughly \SI{124}{kW} installed bus, while a \SI{500}{kg/h} module implies roughly \SI{621}{kW}. Third, for stock-aircraft concepts the first hard limits are \emph{electrical class and unsupported endurance}, not raw process-fuel mass. Process fuel is material, but it is modest compared with propulsion fuel on short sorties; long persistence pushes the concept into auxiliary cargo fuel, receiver-capable seeder assumptions, and tanker-support scaling \citep{AeolusPowerNote,AeolusFuelNote,AeolusAircraftPower}. Fourth, when the same \SI{48}{h} mission architecture is priced using official FY2026 DoD flight-hour rates, the cost swing is dominated by particle diameter rather than by small differences in public C-130 variant hourly rates: the \SI{20}{nm}, \SI{2000}{particles/cm^3} full-ring case is about \SI{4.81}{MUSD} at a \SI{500}{nmi} radius mission, while the \SI{50}{nm} full-ring case is about \SI{74.85}{MUSD} under the same pricing basis \citep{AeolusCostNote,DoDFY2026Rates}.

\vspace{0.25em}
The paper therefore reaches a clear scoping conclusion. A stock or modestly upgraded C-130-family concept looks naturally aligned with the roadmap per-aircraft regime of roughly \SIrange{20}{60}{kg/h}. Once the concept moves into the \SIrange{100}{500}{kg/h} single-aircraft regime, the public references no longer support a stock-C-130-APU interpretation; the relevant analogs become dedicated \SI{200}{kW}, \SI{250}{kW}, and ultimately \SI{1}{MW} mission-power hardware, plus the integration burden that comes with them. The full-ring, \SI{48}{h} intervention problem is therefore best understood as a fleet-architecture and mission-support problem, not simply a question of carrying aerosol mass.

\section*{Motivation and Program Question}

If aerosol-based hurricane intervention is to be taken seriously as an engineering concept, the first requirement is to replace vague statements about ``seeding a storm'' with a reproducible mass-and-mission model. The intervention concept only becomes decision-useful when the team can answer six linked questions with the same set of assumptions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.1em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item What number concentration must be sustained in the storm control volume?
\item How does required mass shift as particle diameter changes?
\item How much aircraft throughput is implied over a \SI{48}{h} campaign?
\item What onboard process power is required to generate that throughput?
\item Can a stock C-130-family aircraft support that burden, or is missionized power hardware required?
\item If the campaign demands long airborne persistence, what tanker support follows?
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.25em}
This paper focuses on that chain. It does \emph{not} claim atmospheric efficacy of the intervention itself, nor does it address operational policy, weather-modification governance, or tactical employment doctrine. Instead, it addresses a narrower and more tractable problem: whether the aircraft-side architecture implied by the NH$_4$Cl seeding concept closes physically under transparent assumptions.

\vspace{0.25em}
The project context also matters. The current program discussion highlights two particle-size bands of practical interest: a laboratory-scale band near \SIrange{5}{10}{nm}, and a broader operational study band near \SIrange{20}{50}{nm}. Because the payload law is cubic in diameter, these bands are not incremental variations of the same problem; they represent qualitatively different aircraft burdens. The methods and figures below are therefore structured to make those regime changes visually obvious.

\section*{System Framing and Objectives}

The system objective used throughout this analysis is to sustain a target aerosol number concentration in a treated storm annulus for \SI{48}{h}. The baseline annulus assumptions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.1em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item storm-ring radius $R = \SI{300}{km}$,
\item treated radial width $W = \SI{50}{km}$,
\item treated-layer depth $H = \SI{2}{km}$,
\item mean radial inflow speed $u = \SI{10}{m/s}$,
\item azimuthal coverage fraction $f = 1.0$ for full circumference and $f = 0.25$ for one quadrant \citep{AeolusPayloadMethods}.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.25em}
The scenario space swept in this analysis is:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.1em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item target concentrations of \SIlist{200;300;500;1000;2000}{particles/cm^3},
\item particle diameters from \SIrange{5}{100}{nm}, with emphasis on \SIrange{5}{10}{nm} and \SIrange{20}{50}{nm},
\item both full-circumference and one-quadrant coverage,
\item aircraft dispersal rates of \SIlist{100;300;500}{kg/h per plane},
\item a stock-C-130 unsupported-sortie interpretation and a receiver-capable seeder interpretation for tanker-supported cases.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.25em}
To keep terminology precise, this paper uses four definitions consistently:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.1em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item \textbf{C-130 20 t load equivalent}: total NH$_4$Cl mass divided by a \SI{20}{t} aerosol payload anchor. This is \emph{not} an aircraft count.
\item \textbf{Concurrent seeders}: the number of aircraft required simultaneously to sustain the mean campaign mass rate at a given per-aircraft dispersal rate.
\item \textbf{Stock internal-fuel proxy}: a planning fuel budget derived from public loaded range and cruise-speed data for the C-130J-30, not a claimed certified tank-capacity number.
\item \textbf{Missionized power hardware}: dedicated airborne generation hardware such as quarter-megawatt generators or megawatt-class turbogenerators, distinct from a stock aircraft APU.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Diameter-based NH$_4$Cl payload model}

For monodisperse spherical NH$_4$Cl particles with diameter $d$ and density $\rho = \SI{1527}{kg/m^3}$, the mass of one particle is
\begin{equation}
m_p = \rho \frac{\pi}{6} d^3 .
\end{equation}
This particle-mass expression is used throughout the payload, power, fuel, tanker-support, and cost models.

\vspace{0.25em}
The treated control volume is a storm annulus,
\begin{equation}
V = f \, 2 \pi R W H ,
\end{equation}
and the volumetric inflow of fresh air through the outer storm boundary is
\begin{equation}
Q = f \, 2 \pi R H u .
\end{equation}
For a target number concentration $C_{\mathrm{cc}}$ in particles per cubic centimeter,
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{m^3}} = 10^6 C_{\mathrm{cc}} .
\end{equation}

\vspace{0.25em}
Over a \SI{48}{h} campaign of duration $T$, total required particle count is the sum of initial fill plus replenishment:
\begin{equation}
N_{\mathrm{total}} = C_{\mathrm{m^3}} (V + QT) .
\end{equation}
The resulting total NH$_4$Cl mass is
\begin{equation}
M_{\mathrm{total}}
= C_{\mathrm{m^3}} \rho \frac{\pi}{6} d^3 f 2 \pi R H (W + uT) .
\end{equation}
This makes the scaling transparent: linear in concentration, coverage fraction, storm radius, and treated-layer depth; cubic in particle diameter; and approximately linear in inflow speed for long campaigns \citep{AeolusPayloadMethods}.

\vspace{0.25em}
The residence time is simply
\begin{equation}
\tau = \frac{V}{Q} = \frac{W}{u} ,
\end{equation}
which is about \SI{1.39}{h} in the baseline case. Because $uT = \SI{1728}{km}$ is much larger than $W = \SI{50}{km}$, replenishment dominates the campaign mass requirement. That is why the figures show much larger continuous-\SI{48}{h} burdens than one-shot fill burdens.

\subsection*{Aircraft interpretation}

The payload model is translated into aircraft terms in two ways. First, the C-130 load-equivalent metric is
\begin{equation}
L_{\mathrm{C130}} = \frac{M_{\mathrm{total}}}{\SI{20000}{kg}} .
\end{equation}
Second, if the mean required campaign mass rate is $\dot{M}$ and each aircraft can disperse at rate $r_{\mathrm{plane}}$, then the number of simultaneous seeders is
\begin{equation}
N_{\mathrm{seeders}} = \left\lceil \frac{\dot{M}}{r_{\mathrm{plane}}} \right\rceil .
\end{equation}
This analysis evaluates $r_{\mathrm{plane}} = \SIlist{100;300;500}{kg/h}$ to show how the same atmospheric requirement maps into very different fleet architectures.

\subsection*{Process power model}

The process-power model is intentionally top-down because the detailed molten-salt process electrical architecture is not yet designed. From the process proposal, the molten-salt basis is taken as a constant specific energy of
\begin{equation}
e = \SI{1.08}{kWh/kg} .
\end{equation}
If the NH$_4$Cl throughput is $\dot{m}$ in \si{kg/h}, the net process electrical load is
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{process}} = e \dot{m} .
\end{equation}
The installed aircraft bus is then modeled with a \SI{15}{\percent} integration margin,
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{bus}} = 1.15 P_{\mathrm{process}} ,
\end{equation}
and the corresponding prime-power thermal input is
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{thermal}} = \frac{P_{\mathrm{bus}}}{0.35} .
\end{equation}
The \SI{15}{\percent} bus margin and \SI{35}{\percent} net electrical efficiency are explicit engineering assumptions, not values quoted from an aircraft OEM source \citep{AeolusPowerNote}.

\subsection*{Stock-C-130 fuel-budget model}

To avoid claiming exact stock C-130 internal fuel capacity without a primary source, the fuel-budget analysis uses a stock unsupported-sortie planning proxy derived from public C-130J-30 performance data. With cruise speed $v = \SI{356}{ktas}$ and range at maximum normal payload $R_{\mathrm{loaded}} = \SI{2100}{nmi}$, the loaded-endurance proxy is
\begin{equation}
t_{\mathrm{loaded}} = \frac{R_{\mathrm{loaded}}}{v} = \SI{5.90}{h} .
\end{equation}
This endurance is combined with a propulsion-fuel-burn uncertainty band of \SIlist{2.8;3.5;4.2}{t/h} to define a stock internal-fuel planning proxy \citep{AeolusFuelNote,LockheedC130J30}.

\vspace{0.25em}
Process fuel is derived from installed bus and jet-fuel lower heating value:
\begin{equation}
\dot{m}_{\mathrm{fuel,process}} = \frac{P_{\mathrm{bus}}}{\eta_{\mathrm{gen}} \,\mathrm{LHV}_{\mathrm{fuel}}} ,
\end{equation}
with $\eta_{\mathrm{gen}} = 0.25/0.35/0.45$ and $\mathrm{LHV}_{\mathrm{fuel}} = \SI{43}{MJ/kg} = \SI{11.94}{kWh/kg}$ \citep{AeolusFuelNote}. Total mission fuel is then the sum of propulsion fuel and process fuel over a sortie duration, and auxiliary cargo-bay fuel is the deficit between that total and the stock internal-fuel proxy.

\subsection*{Aerial-refueling support model}

The advanced tanker-support model asks a different operational question: how much support is required to keep a receiver-capable C-130-family seeder airborne and spraying for a continuous \SI{48}{h} slot? For a storm at distance $D$ from the forward base, the airborne mission duration for one minimal-rotation slot is
\begin{equation}
t_{\mathrm{airborne}} = \SI{48}{h} + \frac{2D}{v_{\mathrm{seed}}} .
\end{equation}
The number of refuel contacts required for one seeder is
\begin{equation}
N_{\mathrm{refuels}} = \left\lceil \frac{t_{\mathrm{airborne}}}{T_{\mathrm{endurance}}} \right\rceil - 1 ,
\end{equation}
and aerial fuel offload required for that seeder is
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{aerial}} = \max(0, t_{\mathrm{airborne}} - T_{\mathrm{endurance}})\,\dot{m}_{\mathrm{fuel,seeder}} .
\end{equation}
The tanker model then scales contacts and offload by the number of simultaneous seeder slots \citep{AeolusPayloadMethods,AeolusSourceLog}.

\vspace{0.25em}
One important caveat: no public primary source was found that imposes a hard maximum number of aerial refuels a C-130 can accept in one mission, so the model reports required contact counts rather than inventing a hard cap \citep{AeolusSourceLog}.

\subsection*{Flight-hour cost model}

The cost model is intentionally simple and tied directly to the seeder+tanker mission logic. It asks what the intervention costs if one prices only the airborne hours implied by the \SI{48}{h} campaign architecture. Using the official FY2026 DoD fixed-wing reimbursable-rate table, the base seeder hourly cost is taken as the Air Force C-130J Other DoD Component user rate and the tanker cost is taken as the KC-130J Other DoD Component user rate \citep{DoDFY2026Rates,AeolusCostNote}. Receiver-capable HC-130J and MC-130J rates are retained as a narrow seeder-cost sensitivity band.

\vspace{0.25em}
For one campaign scenario, seeder flight-hour cost is
\begin{equation}
Cost_{\mathrm{seeder}} =
N_{\mathrm{seeders}} \,
t_{\mathrm{airborne,seeder}} \,
c_{\mathrm{seeder}} ,
\end{equation}
and tanker airborne hours per sortie are
\begin{equation}
t_{\mathrm{airborne,tanker}} =
\frac{2D}{v_{\mathrm{tanker}}}
+
\bar{N}_{\mathrm{contacts}} t_{\mathrm{contact}} .
\end{equation}
The tanker cost is then
\begin{equation}
Cost_{\mathrm{tanker}} =
N_{\mathrm{tanker\,sorties}} \,
t_{\mathrm{airborne,tanker}} \,
c_{\mathrm{tanker}} ,
\end{equation}
so total campaign flight-hour cost is
\begin{equation}
Cost_{\mathrm{total}} = Cost_{\mathrm{seeder}} + Cost_{\mathrm{tanker}} .
\end{equation}
Ground turnaround time is excluded from this pricing layer because the purpose of the model is a \emph{flight-hour proxy}, not a whole-deployment cost estimate \citep{AeolusCostNote}.

\section*{Results: Payload Scaling and State Space}

Figure~\ref{fig:ccnsummary} is the most compact summary of the payload model. Panel (a) shows that full-ring \SI{48}{h} mass rises almost perfectly as the cube of particle diameter, so the difference between \SI{10}{nm}, \SI{20}{nm}, and \SI{50}{nm} is not a modest sensitivity but a regime change. Panel (b) shows why the full-ring continuous-\SI{48}{h} requirement sits far above the one-shot fill curves: under the baseline annulus assumptions, the campaign is replenishment-dominated. Panel (c) converts the same burden into mass-normalized C-130 load equivalents. Panel (d) makes the operational consequence explicit by converting mean campaign mass rate into concurrent aircraft count at \SIlist{100;300;500}{kg/h per plane}. The teal overlay highlights the diameter window where the full-ring, \SI{2000}{particles/cm^3}, \SI{300}{kg/h} case stays within roughly \SIrange{6}{12} simultaneous aircraft.

\section*{C-130/APU Interpretation and Engineering Boundaries}

The C-130 and APU evidence gathered for this analysis requires careful interpretation because several related Honeywell products are easy to conflate. A stock aircraft APU is an auxiliary gas turbine that supports engine start, pneumatic loads, and auxiliary electrical power. A generator is the electrical machine that converts shaft power into electricity. A turbogenerator is a dedicated package that couples a gas-turbine core with a high-power generator. These are related technologies, but they are not interchangeable aircraft architectures \citep{AeolusAircraftPower}.

\vspace{0.25em}
The strongest public C-130-specific clue found is Honeywell's statement that its C-130 APU offering expands generator capability from \SIrange{20}{40}{kVA} \citep{HoneywellC130}. Under the current installed-bus basis, that supports only about \SI{32}{kg/h} of NH$_4$Cl throughput. By contrast, Honeywell's APU overview brochure states that the larger HGT1700 APU family uses a start-generator system providing \SI{150}{kVA} of electrical power \citep{HoneywellAPUBrochure}, which corresponds to about \SI{121}{kg/h} under the same bus basis. The public \SI{250}{kW} generator corresponds to about \SI{201}{kg/h}, while the public \SI{1}{MW} turbogenerator corresponds to about \SI{805}{kg/h} \citep{Honeywell250kW,HoneywellHAL,Honeywell1MWMilestone}.

\vspace{0.25em}
This distinction resolves the apparent contradiction between ``the C-130 APU looks too small'' and ``Honeywell can generate huge electrical power.'' Both statements can be true. For a \emph{stock C-130} interpretation, the APU is a real limiter. For a \emph{missionized aircraft} carrying dedicated generation hardware, the APU is no longer the primary limiter; the relevant constraints become prime mover, fuel flow, high-voltage distribution, cooling, and certification. Public Honeywell material confirms that megawatt-class airborne generation is physically plausible, but it does \emph{not} imply that a stock C-130 electrical system can simply absorb or supply \SI{1}{MW}.

\vspace{0.25em}
The supporting notes on cargo-bay auxiliary fuel and long-endurance precedent reinforce the same systems conclusion. The KC-130 family provides real precedent for removable or palletized internal fuel storage in the cargo compartment and for integrating that fuel into the aircraft mission-fuel architecture \citep{AeolusInternalFuelNote,LockheedKC130Offload}. Public long-endurance C-130-family examples also show that with tanker support, fuel ceases to be the only limiting variable; crew endurance and mission risk tolerance become central \citep{AeolusRefuelEndurance}.

\section*{Program Implications, Limitations, and Next Steps}

The work documented here reduces the intervention concept to a clear design-space statement.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.1em,itemsep=0.15em]
\item If the concept remains near the roadmap per-aircraft band of roughly \SIrange{20}{60}{kg/h}, then a stock-aircraft or modestly missionized C-130-family interpretation remains at least discussable using public-source evidence.
\item If the concept moves into the \SIrange{100}{500}{kg/h} single-aircraft band, then the problem shifts into dedicated mission-power territory. In that regime, the relevant public references are quarter-megawatt generators and megawatt-class turbogenerators, not a stock C-130 APU.
\item If the concept demands \SI{48}{h} continuous full-ring persistence at the higher concentration targets, then the burden rapidly becomes a fleet-size, tanker-support, and multi-million-dollar campaign problem even when aerosol payload per airplane is manageable.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.25em}
Several limitations remain explicit and should stay explicit. The paper does not include a bottom-up electrical load list for the molten-salt system; it uses the current top-down \SI{1.08}{kWh/kg} basis because the detailed reactor/process electrical design is not yet fixed. The stock-fuel model uses a public performance-derived proxy rather than a certified configuration fuel number. The refueling model includes engineering assumptions for seeder fuel burn, tanker contact time, and sortie-contact limits where public primary sources were not available. None of those are hidden in the paper; they are part of the reproducible record.

\vspace{0.25em}
The next engineering steps are now well defined. First, the program needs to decide whether it is pursuing a distributed low-rate architecture or a smaller number of higher-rate missionized aircraft, because that choice dominates the power-plant problem. Second, any higher-rate concept needs a dedicated mission-power architecture study rather than more discussion of stock-APU plausibility. Third, if persistent operations remain in scope, the tanker and crew-concept side of the mission needs to be treated as a first-class design variable rather than a late-stage logistics add-on.
